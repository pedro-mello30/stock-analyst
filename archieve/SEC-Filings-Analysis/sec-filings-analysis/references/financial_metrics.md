# Key Financial Ratios and Metrics

This reference provides comprehensive definitions and formulas for key financial ratios used in SEC filings analysis.

## Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios measure a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations.

### Current Ratio
**Formula**: Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

**Purpose**: Measures ability to pay short-term obligations
**Industry Standard**: 1.5-3.0
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate better liquidity, but too high may suggest inefficient asset usage

### Quick Ratio (Acid-Test)
**Formula**: (Current Assets - Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities

**Purpose**: More stringent liquidity measure excluding inventory
**Industry Standard**: 1.0-2.0
**Interpretation**: Better indicator of immediate liquidity position

### Cash Ratio
**Formula**: (Cash + Cash Equivalents) ÷ Current Liabilities

**Purpose**: Most conservative liquidity measure
**Industry Standard**: 0.2-0.5
**Interpretation**: Shows ability to cover liabilities with cash alone

## Profitability Ratios

Profitability ratios measure a company's ability to generate earnings relative to revenue, assets, and equity.

### Gross Margin
**Formula**: (Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold) ÷ Revenue

**Purpose**: Measures production efficiency and pricing power
**Industry Standard**: Varies by industry (10-60%)
**Interpretation**: Higher margins indicate better cost control or pricing power

### Operating Margin
**Formula**: Operating Income ÷ Revenue

**Purpose**: Measures operating efficiency before interest and taxes
**Industry Standard**: 5-20%
**Interpretation**: Shows core business profitability

### Net Margin
**Formula**: Net Income ÷ Revenue

**Purpose**: Overall profitability after all expenses
**Industry Standard**: 2-15%
**Interpretation**: Final measure of profitability efficiency

### Return on Assets (ROA)
**Formula**: Net Income ÷ Total Assets

**Purpose**: Measures asset utilization efficiency
**Industry Standard**: 5-10%
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate better asset management

### Return on Equity (ROE)
**Formula**: Net Income ÷ Shareholder's Equity

**Purpose**: Measures return on shareholder investment
**Industry Standard**: 10-15%
**Interpretation**: Key metric for investors, shows equity efficiency

## Efficiency Ratios

Efficiency ratios measure how well a company uses its assets and manages its operations.

### Asset Turnover
**Formula**: Revenue ÷ Total Assets

**Purpose**: Measures asset utilization for revenue generation
**Industry Standard**: 0.5-2.0
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate more efficient asset use

### Inventory Turnover
**Formula**: Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Average Inventory

**Purpose**: Measures inventory management efficiency
**Industry Standard**: 4-10
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate faster inventory movement

### Receivables Turnover
**Formula**: Revenue ÷ Average Accounts Receivable

**Purpose**: Measures collection efficiency
**Industry Standard**: 6-12
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate faster collection

### Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)
**Formula**: (Accounts Receivable ÷ Revenue) × 365

**Purpose**: Average days to collect receivables
**Industry Standard**: 30-60 days
**Interpretation**: Lower values indicate better collection practices

## Leverage Ratios

Leverage ratios measure the degree of debt financing and financial risk.

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio
**Formula**: Total Debt ÷ Total Equity

**Purpose**: Measures financial leverage
**Industry Standard**: 0.5-2.0
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate more debt financing and higher risk

### Debt-to-Assets Ratio
**Formula**: Total Debt ÷ Total Assets

**Purpose**: Measures asset financing through debt
**Industry Standard**: 0.3-0.6
**Interpretation**: Shows proportion of assets financed by debt

### Interest Coverage Ratio
**Formula**: EBIT ÷ Interest Expense

**Purpose**: Measures ability to service debt
**Industry Standard**: 3.0-5.0
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate better debt servicing ability

### Financial Leverage
**Formula**: Total Assets ÷ Total Equity

**Purpose**: Measures equity multiplier effect
**Industry Standard**: 1.5-3.0
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate more leverage

## Market Ratios

Market ratios measure shareholder value and market perception.

### Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
**Formula**: Market Price per Share ÷ Earnings per Share

**Purpose**: Measures market expectations
**Industry Standard**: Varies significantly
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate growth expectations

### Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio
**Formula**: Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share

**Purpose**: Compares market value to book value
**Industry Standard**: 1.0-3.0
**Interpretation**: Values above 1 indicate market premium over book value

### Dividend Yield
**Formula**: Annual Dividends per Share ÷ Market Price per Share

**Purpose**: Measures dividend return
**Industry Standard**: 2-4%
**Interpretation**: Higher yields attractive to income investors

### Earnings Per Share (EPS)
**Formula**: Net Income ÷ Outstanding Shares

**Purpose**: Measures profitability per share
**Industry Standard**: Varies by company
**Interpretation**: Key metric for equity valuation

## Cash Flow Ratios

Cash flow ratios measure cash generation and liquidity.

### Operating Cash Flow Ratio
**Formula**: Operating Cash Flow ÷ Current Liabilities

**Purpose**: Measures cash flow coverage of short-term debt
**Industry Standard**: 1.0+
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate better cash liquidity

### Cash Flow Coverage Ratio
**Formula**: Operating Cash Flow ÷ Total Debt

**Purpose**: Measures ability to service total debt with cash flow
**Industry Standard**: 0.5-1.0
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate better debt coverage

### Free Cash Flow Yield
**Formula**: (Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures) ÷ Market Capitalization

**Purpose**: Measures cash return to shareholders
**Industry Standard**: 4-8%
**Interpretation**: Higher values indicate better cash generation

## Industry-Specific Ratios

### Retail: Same-Store Sales Growth
**Formula**: (Current Period Sales - Prior Period Sales) ÷ Prior Period Sales

### Technology: Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
**Formula**: Total Sales & Marketing Expenses ÷ New Customers Acquired

### Real Estate: Occupancy Rate
**Formula**: (Occupied Units ÷ Total Units) × 100

### Banking: Loan-to-Deposit Ratio
**Formula**: Total Loans ÷ Total Deposits

## Ratio Analysis Guidelines

### Trend Analysis
- Compare ratios over multiple periods
- Look for consistent patterns
- Identify improving or deteriorating trends

### Peer Comparison
- Compare with industry averages
- Analyze against key competitors
- Understand industry-specific benchmarks

### Ratio Relationships
- Liquidity and leverage are often inversely related
- Profitability and efficiency typically correlate positively
- High growth often comes with lower current profitability

### Limitations
- Ratios vary significantly by industry
- Accounting methods affect ratio calculations
- Seasonal factors can distort ratios
- One-time events may skew results

## Interpretation Framework

### Excellent Performance
- Liquidity ratios above industry average
- Profitability ratios in top quartile
- Efficiency ratios showing improvement
- Leverage ratios within safe limits

### Warning Signs
- Declining liquidity ratios
- Shrinking profit margins
- Increasing leverage
- Deteriorating efficiency metrics

### Context Matters
- Consider economic conditions
- Account for company lifecycle stage
- Factor in strategic initiatives
- Assess management quality

This reference serves as a foundation for financial analysis and should be used in conjunction with qualitative assessment and industry knowledge.
